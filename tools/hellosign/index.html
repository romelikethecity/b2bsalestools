The Bottom Line

Dropbox Sign is the right e-signature tool for two audiences: developers embedding signatures into their products and cost-conscious teams that want simple, affordable signing. The API is the cleanest in the category, and a developer can go from documentation to working integration faster than with any competitor. The signing experience is smooth for recipients. The pricing saves 30-40% compared to DocuSign.

The product has coasted since the Dropbox acquisition. Feature development has slowed while competitors have shipped AI analysis, advanced workflows, and deeper integrations. Dropbox Sign today is the same solid product it was two years ago, which is fine if that product meets your needs but concerning if you expect your e-signature tool to grow with you. Teams that start on Dropbox Sign may find themselves evaluating DocuSign or PandaDoc as requirements expand.

Choose Dropbox Sign if you need an API-first e-signature solution, you want to save money versus DocuSign on basic signing needs, or you're a Dropbox-standardized organization that wants integrated document storage. Look elsewhere if you need advanced CRM integration (DocuSign), free e-signatures (PandaDoc), full contract lifecycle management (Ironclad, Juro), or a platform that's innovating aggressively in the e-signature space. Dropbox Sign does the basics well at a fair price, and for many teams, that's all they need.

Dropbox Sign (HelloSign) Overview

Dropbox Sign, formerly HelloSign, is the e-signature tool built for simplicity and developer friendliness. While DocuSign chases enterprise complexity and Ironclad tackles full contract lifecycle management, Dropbox Sign stays focused on making e-signatures easy to send, easy to sign, and easy to integrate. The product does fewer things than its competitors, but it does them with less friction. For teams that want to send documents for signature without wading through feature menus they'll never use, that focus is appealing.

The developer story is where Dropbox Sign historically separated itself. The API was built to be clean, well-documented, and easy to implement. Companies embedding e-signature into their own products (SaaS platforms, marketplaces, HR tools) gravitated to HelloSign's API because it worked in hours instead of weeks. The API documentation reads like it was written by developers for developers, not by a product marketing team. Dropbox has maintained this developer focus since the acquisition, and the API pricing remains competitive with DocuSign's API plans.

The signing experience for recipients is clean and fast. No account creation required. The signer gets an email, opens the document, clicks through the signature fields, and they're done. The interface strips away unnecessary chrome and focuses on getting the document signed with minimum clicks. For B2B sales teams sending contracts to prospects who may be unfamiliar with e-signature tools, this simplicity reduces drop-off during the signing step.

The Dropbox acquisition brought tighter integration with Dropbox storage and workflow tools but also raised questions about Dropbox Sign's independence as a product. Feature development has slowed compared to the HelloSign days. The product is solid for standard e-signature needs but hasn't added the AI capabilities, advanced workflow features, or deep CRM integrations that competitors have been shipping. If you need basic e-signatures with a great API, Dropbox Sign delivers. If you need the broader platform capabilities that DocuSign, PandaDoc, or Juro offer, Dropbox Sign will leave you wanting more.

Pros & Cons

  • Best-in-class API for embedded e-signaturesDropbox Sign's API is clean, well-documented, and fast to implement. A developer can integrate e-signature into a web application in a few hours. The API handles template creation, document generation, signature collection, and webhook notifications. For SaaS companies building e-signature into their product, the developer experience is superior to DocuSign's API, which is more powerful but significantly more complex to implement.
  • Clean, fast signing experienceRecipients sign documents quickly without creating an account, downloading an app, or navigating complex interfaces. The signing flow is streamlined: open email, review document, click signature fields, done. Average signing time is under 2 minutes for simple documents. For sales teams worried about prospects dropping off during the signing step, Dropbox Sign's minimal-friction experience helps maintain completion rates.
  • Straightforward pricing with no hidden costsAt $15/user/month for Essentials and $25/user/month for Standard, Dropbox Sign is 30-40% cheaper than DocuSign's equivalent tiers. The pricing is transparent with no envelope limits on paid plans. What you see on the website is what you pay. No surprise overages, no feature gates hidden behind enterprise-only tiers. For cost-conscious teams, this pricing clarity is refreshing.
  • Dropbox integration for document storageSigned documents automatically save to Dropbox for teams using Dropbox as their file storage. This creates a clean archive of executed agreements organized by folder structure, without manual downloads and uploads. For organizations standardized on Dropbox for file management, this integration is a natural fit that eliminates a step from the post-signature workflow.
  • Feature development has slowed since the Dropbox acquisitionHelloSign was shipping new features regularly before the Dropbox acquisition. Since then, the pace has slowed. Competitors have added AI-powered contract analysis, advanced approval workflows, and deeper CRM integrations while Dropbox Sign's feature set has remained relatively static. The core product is stable, but teams looking for innovation in the e-signature space will find it elsewhere.
  • CRM integrations are basic compared to DocuSignDropbox Sign integrates with Salesforce and HubSpot, but the integrations lack the depth of DocuSign for Salesforce. Auto-populating fields from CRM records, triggering automated workflows based on signature events, and syncing detailed status data back to the CRM are all more limited. Sales ops teams that want tight CRM-signature integration will find Dropbox Sign's connections functional but thin.
  • Brand recognition is lower than DocuSignWhen a prospect receives a Dropbox Sign envelope, they may not recognize the brand as readily as DocuSign. The rename from HelloSign to Dropbox Sign helped (Dropbox is well-known) but the product doesn't carry the same 'I've signed this before' familiarity. In enterprise B2B sales, where signers may be cautious about unfamiliar signing tools, this recognition gap can add friction.
  • Template and workflow capabilities are limitedDropbox Sign's templates handle basic documents well but lack conditional logic, dynamic fields, and complex routing rules. If you need documents that adapt based on signer inputs, multi-stage approval workflows before sending, or sophisticated field validation, Dropbox Sign will feel restrictive. DocuSign and PandaDoc offer meaningfully more template flexibility.

Use Cases

SaaS Company Embedding E-Signatures in Their Product

A B2B SaaS platform that manages vendor agreements for procurement teams embeds Dropbox Sign's API to let users send contracts for signature directly from the platform. The integration took one developer three days to build. When a procurement manager finalizes vendor terms in the platform, the system generates a contract from a template via the API, populates it with deal data, and sends it to the vendor for signature. Webhook notifications update the deal status in real time when the vendor signs. The platform processes 2,000 signature requests per month through the API. Dropbox Sign's API pricing is $600/month for this volume, compared to an estimated $1,200/month on DocuSign's API plan.

Small Sales Team Needing Affordable E-Signatures

An 8-person sales team at a bootstrapped B2B company needs e-signatures for proposals and contracts. DocuSign's Business Pro at $40/user/month ($3,840/year) is over budget. The team chooses Dropbox Sign Essentials at $15/user/month ($1,440/year), saving $2,400/year. Reps create 5 templates for standard agreements, send proposals for signature, and track completion status. The signing experience is clean enough that clients complete documents without confusion. The team gives up advanced analytics, detailed Salesforce integration, and template conditional logic, but none of those features were requirements at this stage.

HR Department Managing Employee Onboarding Documents

A 200-person company uses Dropbox Sign to handle new hire paperwork. Offer letters, NDAs, benefits enrollment forms, and I-9 supplements are all templated. When a new hire starts, HR generates and sends the complete onboarding packet in under 5 minutes. The new hire signs everything from their phone before their first day. Signed documents save automatically to a shared Dropbox folder organized by employee name. HR processed 45 new hires last quarter without printing a single page. The total cost is $25/month for one HR coordinator on the Standard plan, handling documents that previously consumed 3 hours of HR time per new hire in paper-based onboarding.

Pricing Analysis

Dropbox Sign offers three tiers. Essentials is $15/user/month (billed annually) and includes unlimited signature requests, templates, audit trails, and basic integrations. Standard is $25/user/month and adds team features, branding customization, bulk sending, and Salesforce integration. Premium pricing is custom and includes advanced security, compliance features, and dedicated support.

API plans are priced separately and are competitive with DocuSign's API offerings. The API is available as an add-on to business plans or as a standalone product for companies embedding e-signatures into their own applications. API pricing scales with volume, starting around $150/month for lower-volume use cases.

The pricing advantage over DocuSign is clear at every tier. Dropbox Sign Essentials ($15) matches most features in DocuSign Standard ($25). Dropbox Sign Standard ($25) covers much of what DocuSign Business Pro ($40) offers. The savings compound with team size. A 20-person team saves $4,800-$7,200/year by choosing Dropbox Sign over equivalent DocuSign plans. The trade-off is fewer enterprise features, simpler CRM integrations, and lower brand recognition.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is HelloSign now Dropbox Sign?

Yes. Dropbox acquired HelloSign in 2019 and rebranded it to Dropbox Sign in 2022. The product is the same with ongoing development under the Dropbox brand. If you see references to HelloSign in documentation, reviews, or forums, they're referring to what is now Dropbox Sign. The API retains some HelloSign naming conventions for backward compatibility.

How does Dropbox Sign compare to DocuSign?

Dropbox Sign is simpler, cheaper, and has a better developer API. DocuSign is more feature-rich, has deeper CRM integrations, and carries stronger brand recognition. For teams that need basic e-signatures at a good price, Dropbox Sign wins. For enterprise sales teams with complex routing, Salesforce-heavy workflows, and compliance requirements, DocuSign is the safer choice. Dropbox Sign saves 30-40% on per-seat costs across comparable tiers.

Is Dropbox Sign free?

Dropbox Sign offers a free trial but no permanent free tier for sending documents. You can sign documents others send you for free. For sending documents for signature, the cheapest option is Essentials at $15/user/month. If free e-signature sending is a requirement, PandaDoc offers a completely free plan with unlimited signatures.

Does Dropbox Sign integrate with Salesforce?

Yes, on the Standard plan ($25/user/month) and above. The integration lets you send documents for signature from Salesforce records and track signing status. However, the integration is less mature than DocuSign for Salesforce. Auto-field population, advanced status tracking, and workflow automation are more limited. Sales teams heavily reliant on Salesforce for contract management should evaluate whether the integration meets their specific needs.

Is Dropbox Sign secure and legally binding?

Yes. Dropbox Sign signatures comply with the ESIGN Act, UETA, and eIDAS regulations. Every signature includes a tamper-evident audit trail with timestamps, IP addresses, and authentication details. The platform is SOC 2 Type II certified and HIPAA compliant. Signed documents are encrypted in transit and at rest. Security and legal validity are on par with DocuSign and other major e-signature providers.
