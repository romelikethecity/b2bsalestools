The Bottom Line

Adobe Acrobat Sign is the right e-signature tool in two specific scenarios: you're a Microsoft 365 organization that wants e-signatures embedded in Outlook, Word, and Teams, or you already have Adobe enterprise licenses and adding e-signature is a marginal cost decision. In either case, Acrobat Sign delivers solid e-signature functionality without a new vendor evaluation or significant incremental spend.

Outside those scenarios, Acrobat Sign faces tough competition. DocuSign wins on Salesforce integration, template sophistication, and sales-specific features. PandaDoc wins on free e-signatures and proposal integration. Ironclad and Juro win on full contract lifecycle management. Acrobat Sign doesn't lead any of those specific categories, but it's competent across all of them, and it handles complex PDF documents better than anyone.

Choose Acrobat Sign if your company already pays Adobe and you want to avoid adding another vendor. Choose it if Microsoft 365 integration matters more than Salesforce integration. Choose it if you deal with complex, graphic-heavy PDFs that other tools struggle to render correctly. For everyone else, DocuSign offers more sales-specific value, and PandaDoc offers better pricing for small teams.

Adobe Acrobat Sign Overview

Adobe Acrobat Sign is the e-signature tool for organizations already embedded in Adobe's ecosystem. If your team lives in Adobe Acrobat, Creative Cloud, or Microsoft 365 (where Adobe has deep integration), Acrobat Sign fits into existing workflows without forcing anyone to learn a new tool. The PDF handling is predictably excellent. Creating, editing, and signing PDFs all happen in the same environment, which eliminates the export-import friction that plagues other e-signature workflows.

The product covers standard e-signature needs competently. You can send documents for signature, set signing order, add form fields, create reusable templates, and collect payments. The web interface works, the mobile app is functional, and the API supports custom integrations. Acrobat Sign also supports digital signatures (certificate-based, not just electronic) for industries and regions that require them, particularly in the EU and regulated sectors.

Where Acrobat Sign earns its keep is the Microsoft integration. It embeds directly into Microsoft Word, Outlook, Teams, and SharePoint. A rep can prepare a contract in Word, add signature fields, and send it for signature without leaving the application. For organizations standardized on Microsoft 365, this is a meaningful workflow advantage. The Salesforce integration exists but isn't as polished as DocuSign's. Adobe has improved it over the years, but it still feels like a secondary priority compared to the Microsoft connection.

The limitation is that Adobe treats Acrobat Sign as part of a larger ecosystem, not a standalone product. The pricing bundles it with Acrobat Pro features, and the product roadmap often prioritizes Adobe's broader document cloud strategy over e-signature-specific innovation. If you compare Acrobat Sign feature-for-feature against DocuSign, it falls behind on advanced routing, template flexibility, and the breadth of CRM integrations. But if your company already pays for Adobe Acrobat Pro and you need e-signatures, Acrobat Sign is already included in many plans, making it effectively free for those users.

Pros & Cons

  • Best-in-class PDF handlingNo one does PDFs better than Adobe. Creating, editing, converting, compressing, and signing PDFs all happen natively in the same tool. You can add signature fields to complex PDF documents (architectural plans, government forms, multi-page contracts) without reformatting issues. Competitors that convert documents to their own format sometimes break formatting. Acrobat Sign preserves every pixel.
  • Deep Microsoft 365 integrationAcrobat Sign embeds into Word, Outlook, Teams, and SharePoint. You can prepare and send documents for signature from within Outlook, convert Word docs to signable PDFs in one click, and manage signing workflows from Teams. For Microsoft-standardized organizations, this reduces context switching. Your team doesn't need to learn a new tool because the signing capabilities live inside tools they already use daily.
  • Digital signatures for regulated industriesAcrobat Sign supports certificate-based digital signatures in addition to standard electronic signatures. This matters in the EU (eIDAS compliance), healthcare, government, and financial services where certain document types require higher assurance levels. DocuSign offers this too, but Adobe's long history with PDF standards (they created the format) gives them credibility with compliance teams evaluating digital signature solutions.
  • Often bundled with existing Adobe licensesMany organizations already pay for Acrobat Pro subscriptions ($22.99/month). Acrobat Sign is included in or easily added to these plans. If your company has an enterprise Adobe agreement, you may already have access to e-signature capabilities at no incremental cost. This makes the ROI comparison against standalone e-signature tools very favorable.
  • Salesforce integration lags behind DocuSignAcrobat Sign integrates with Salesforce, but the integration is less mature and less feature-rich than DocuSign for Salesforce. Auto-populating fields from Salesforce records, tracking signature status, and triggering workflows are all possible but require more configuration effort and produce a clunkier user experience. Sales teams heavily invested in Salesforce will find DocuSign's integration superior.
  • The product plays second fiddle to Acrobat in Adobe's strategyAdobe's primary focus is the Creative Cloud and document management ecosystem, not e-signature as a standalone category. Product updates and innovation in Acrobat Sign come slower than DocuSign or newer competitors like Juro. You won't see the same pace of feature releases, AI capabilities, or sales-specific functionality that dedicated e-signature companies ship.
  • Confusing plan structure and pricingAdobe's pricing mixes Acrobat features with e-signature capabilities across multiple plans. Figuring out which plan includes which e-signature features requires careful reading. The $12.99/month Acrobat Standard plan includes basic e-signatures. Acrobat Pro at $22.99/month adds more capabilities. Business plans start at $14.99/user/month. The overlap between personal and business plans creates confusion for procurement teams.
  • Template and workflow capabilities are basicAcrobat Sign's templates work for straightforward documents but lack the advanced conditional logic, branching, and dynamic field population that DocuSign Business Pro offers. Complex multi-party signing workflows with conditional routing are harder to set up. Teams that send highly customized agreements with variable sections will feel the limitations compared to DocuSign or PandaDoc.

Use Cases

Microsoft-Centric Enterprise Standardizing on Adobe

A 500-person company standardized on Microsoft 365 and Adobe Acrobat Pro adds e-signature capabilities through their existing Adobe enterprise agreement at minimal incremental cost. Employees send contracts, HR documents, and vendor agreements for signature directly from Microsoft Outlook and Word. The IT team manages everything through the Adobe Admin Console alongside Creative Cloud licenses. No new vendor evaluation, no new security review, no new procurement process. The company sends 300 documents for signature per month across sales, HR, legal, and operations. Since the e-signature capability was already bundled in their Adobe license, they avoided spending $15,000+/year on a standalone tool.

Architecture or Engineering Firm Signing Complex PDF Documents

A 30-person architecture firm sends project proposals, change orders, and compliance documents that include detailed floor plans, technical drawings, and multi-page specifications. These documents are inherently PDF-heavy and formatting matters. Acrobat Sign handles these complex PDFs without the rendering issues that occur when other e-signature tools try to process large, graphic-intensive files. The firm creates templates for standard project agreements and AIA contract forms. Partners sign from their iPads on job sites. The mobile experience handles large PDFs well because Adobe optimized for this exact use case over decades.

European Company Needing eIDAS-Compliant Digital Signatures

A financial services company operating across the EU needs both electronic and qualified electronic signatures to comply with eIDAS regulations. Standard electronic signatures work for most internal documents, but client-facing financial agreements require qualified electronic signatures with certificate-based authentication. Acrobat Sign supports both levels within the same platform, connected to qualified trust service providers across EU member states. The legal team configured document types to automatically require the appropriate signature level based on contract category. This dual capability eliminated the need for separate tools for different signature types.

Pricing Analysis

Adobe Acrobat Sign pricing is intertwined with Acrobat plans. Acrobat Standard at $12.99/month includes basic e-signature capabilities (send for signature, track status, store signed documents). Acrobat Pro at $22.99/month adds advanced e-signature features, PDF editing, and conversion tools. For teams, Acrobat Pro for teams runs $22.99/user/month with admin controls.

Business-specific Acrobat Sign plans start at $14.99/user/month (billed annually) for small business needs. Enterprise pricing requires a sales conversation and typically bundles Acrobat Sign with other Adobe enterprise products. Volume discounts are available at 10+ seats.

The most cost-effective path is often through existing Adobe enterprise agreements. If your company already has Acrobat Pro licenses, adding e-signature capabilities may cost nothing or require a minimal plan upgrade. Check with your Adobe account representative before purchasing Acrobat Sign separately. For companies without existing Adobe licenses, the pricing is competitive with DocuSign's Standard tier but with the added benefit of getting Acrobat's PDF tools included.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Adobe Acrobat Sign the same as Adobe Sign?

Yes. Adobe rebranded Adobe Sign to Adobe Acrobat Sign in 2022 to align it with the Acrobat product family. The product is the same. If you see references to Adobe Sign, Adobe Acrobat Sign, or Adobe Document Cloud e-signatures, they all refer to the same e-signature capability. The rebranding reflects Adobe's strategy of consolidating document tools under the Acrobat brand.

How does Adobe Acrobat Sign compare to DocuSign?

DocuSign leads in sales-specific features, Salesforce integration depth, and third-party integration breadth. Adobe Acrobat Sign leads in PDF handling, Microsoft 365 integration, and value for companies already on Adobe enterprise agreements. For sales teams on Salesforce, DocuSign is typically the stronger choice. For Microsoft-centric organizations with existing Adobe licenses, Acrobat Sign offers better value and workflow integration.

Can I use Adobe Acrobat Sign for free?

Adobe offers a free trial but no permanent free tier for sending documents. You can sign documents others send you for free using the Acrobat Reader app. For sending documents for signature, you need at least an Acrobat Standard subscription ($12.99/month). If you need free e-signature sending, PandaDoc offers a permanently free plan.

Does Adobe Acrobat Sign integrate with Salesforce?

Yes. Adobe offers a Salesforce integration that lets you send and track documents from within Salesforce records. However, the integration is less polished than DocuSign for Salesforce. Field mapping, status tracking, and workflow automation all work but require more manual configuration. Sales teams that rely heavily on Salesforce for contract management typically find DocuSign's integration more capable.

Is Adobe Acrobat Sign compliant with eIDAS?

Yes. Acrobat Sign supports all three eIDAS signature levels: simple electronic signatures, advanced electronic signatures, and qualified electronic signatures through connected qualified trust service providers across EU member states. This makes it one of the few e-signature platforms that can handle the full spectrum of EU signature requirements in a single tool.
